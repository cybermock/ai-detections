# AI-020 - DLP Violation On AI Service Upload

> **Detection ID:** AI-020
> **Status:** Active
> **Risk Score:** 60 (user) + 30 (system) = 90 total
> **MITRE ATT&CK:** T1567.002 - Exfiltration Over Web Service: Exfiltration to Cloud Storage (Exfiltration)
> **Data Model:** Data_Loss_Prevention
> **Schedule:** `*/5 * * * *` (every 5 minutes)
> **Lookback Window:** `-15m@m` to `now`
> **Kill Chain Phase:** Actions on Objectives
> **Last Updated:** 2026-02-13

---

## Overview

This is the highest-confidence detection in the AI RBA pack. It fires when a Data Loss Prevention (DLP) policy violation occurs and the destination is a known AI service provider. This means the organization's DLP system has already identified sensitive or regulated data leaving the network, and that data is being sent to an external AI service.

The business risk is severe: proprietary source code, customer PII, financial data, trade secrets, or regulated information (HIPAA, PCI, SOX) could be uploaded to a third-party AI model where the organization loses control of it. Depending on the AI provider's terms of service, uploaded data may be used for model training, stored indefinitely, or exposed to other users.

This detection produces the highest individual risk score (90 combined) in the pack because both the DLP system and the AI domain correlation independently confirm the risk. A single firing of this detection is sufficient to push a user past the medium risk threshold (60) and close to the high threshold (90).

Common actors include well-meaning employees pasting proprietary code into ChatGPT for debugging, sales teams uploading customer lists for analysis, or in worst-case scenarios, a malicious insider systematically exfiltrating data through AI services to bypass traditional exfiltration controls.

## Detection Logic

### Plain English

1. Query the `Data_Loss_Prevention` data model for all DLP events where a destination is present.
2. Extract the domain from the DLP destination field using regex (strips protocol, port, path).
3. Enrich the domain against `ai_provider_domains.csv` via the `ai_domains_filter` macro to identify known AI providers.
4. Filter to only events where a known AI provider was matched.
5. Aggregate by user, source, and provider: count violations, collect file names, DLP policy types, and AI domains.
6. Exclude sanctioned users/systems via `ai_unsanctioned_filter`.
7. Tag the event with `detection_id=AI-020`.

### Key Fields

| Field | Description | Example Value |
|---|---|---|
| `user` | User who triggered the DLP violation | `jsmith@corp.com` |
| `src` | Source endpoint of the upload | `WORKSTATION-42` |
| `provider` | AI service provider name | `OpenAI` |
| `violations` | Number of DLP violations | `3` |
| `files` | File names involved | `quarterly_report.xlsx, customer_data.csv` |
| `policy_types` | DLP policy types triggered | `PII, PCI, Source Code` |
| `ai_domains` | AI domains data was sent to | `chatgpt.com, api.openai.com` |

### Thresholds

This detection has no configurable thresholds. Any DLP violation directed at an AI service triggers the alert. The DLP system's own policies serve as the first-stage threshold.

## Triage Steps

> **Estimated triage time:** 15-30 minutes

### Step 1: Validate the alert and assess severity

```spl
index=risk search_name="AI RBA - DLP Violation On AI Service Upload" risk_object="$user$" earliest=-24h
| table _time risk_object risk_score search_name
| sort -_time
```

- Confirm the user identity is resolved (not "unknown").
- Note the number of times this detection has fired for this user in the past 24 hours.
- A single firing = 90 risk points, which already exceeds the medium threshold.

### Step 2: Identify the user and their sensitivity level

```spl
| inputlookup identity_lookup_expanded
| search identity="$user$"
| table identity first_name last_name email managedBy category priority bunit
```

- Determine the user's department, role, and manager.
- Flag if the user is in a sensitive department (Finance, Legal, HR, Executive, R&D).
- Flag if the user handles regulated data (HIPAA, PCI, SOX).

### Step 3: Review the DLP violation details

```spl
| datamodel Data_Loss_Prevention search
| search DLP.user="$user$" DLP.dest="*"
| eval ai_domain=lower(coalesce(DLP.dest,""))
| lookup ai_provider_domains domain as ai_domain OUTPUTNEW provider
| where isnotnull(provider)
| table _time DLP.user DLP.src DLP.dest DLP.file_name DLP.file_path DLP.dlp_type DLP.action provider
| sort -_time
```

- **What data was involved?** Check `file_name` and `dlp_type` to understand the sensitivity.
- **What DLP policy was triggered?** PII policies indicate personal data; PCI indicates payment card data.
- **Was the upload blocked or only logged?** Check `DLP.action` - "block" means the data was stopped; "log" or "monitor" means it was allowed through.
- **How many files were involved?** Multiple files suggest intentional bulk exfiltration.

### Step 4: Examine the upload destination and volume

```spl
| tstats summariesonly=t count sum(Web.bytes_out) as bytes_out from datamodel=Web
  where Web.user="$user$" Web.url_domain IN ("chatgpt.com","api.openai.com","claude.ai","api.anthropic.com","gemini.google.com") earliest=-24h
| eval mb_out=round(bytes_out/1024/1024,2)
| table count mb_out
```

- Determine the total data volume uploaded to AI services.
- Cross-reference with the DLP violation count to understand the scope.

### Step 5: Check for related detections

```spl
index=risk risk_object="$user$" earliest=-24h
| stats sum(risk_score) as total_risk values(search_name) as detections dc(search_name) as detection_count by risk_object
| sort -total_risk
```

- Look for overlapping detections: AI-007 (high volume upload) often fires alongside AI-020.
- AI-015 (after-hours) or AI-016 (privileged account) compounds the severity.
- Check total accumulated risk score.

### Step 6: Investigate the user's recent AI activity timeline

```spl
index=risk risk_object="$user$" search_name="AI RBA - *" earliest=-7d
| timechart span=1h sum(risk_score) by search_name
```

- Is this a one-time event or part of a pattern?
- Is the user's AI activity increasing over time?
- Was there a sudden spike in activity?

### Step 7: Determine if data was actually exfiltrated

- If DLP action was "block": data was prevented from leaving. Risk is lower but the intent remains concerning.
- If DLP action was "monitor" or "allow": data likely reached the AI service. Treat as confirmed exfiltration.
- Contact the DLP team for full content inspection results if available.

### Step 8: Make a determination

- **True Positive (Critical):** Sensitive data confirmed uploaded to unsanctioned AI service. Escalate immediately.
- **True Positive (Medium):** DLP policy triggered but data was blocked. User needs education and policy enforcement.
- **Benign True Positive:** User is authorized for AI usage and data type is non-sensitive (rare for DLP+AI correlation).
- **False Positive:** DLP destination parsing incorrectly matched an AI domain (check domain extraction logic).

---

## True Positive Indicators

- DLP policy type is PII, PCI, PHI, source code, or confidential.
- User is not in an approved AI pilot program.
- Multiple files or repeated violations suggest intentional behavior.
- After-hours activity (AI-015 also fired).
- User recently downloaded or accessed sensitive repositories/databases before the upload.
- DLP action was "monitor" (data was not blocked).

---

## False Positive Scenarios

| Scenario | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| DLP destination field contains a CDN domain that resolves to an AI provider | Low | Verify domain extraction regex in the search |
| DLP triggers on non-sensitive data that happens to match a broad policy | Medium | Review the DLP policy specificity; coordinate with DLP team |
| Authorized AI researcher using approved tools with proper data handling | Low | Add to `ai_sanctioned_entities.csv` with documented approval |
| DLP fires on AI service metadata/cookies rather than user-uploaded content | Low | Check file_name and bytes_out to confirm actual content upload |

## Response Actions

### If confirmed True Positive

1. **Contain immediately:**
   - Block the user's access to the AI service at the proxy/firewall level.
   - If the user has API keys for the AI provider, work with IT to revoke them.
   - Consider disabling the user's network access if exfiltration is ongoing or the data is highly sensitive.

2. **Notify stakeholders:**
   - Inform the user's direct manager.
   - Notify the Data Governance / Privacy team.
   - If regulated data is involved (PII, PHI, PCI), notify the Compliance team for potential breach assessment.
   - If source code was uploaded, notify the Engineering Security team.

3. **Investigate data exposure:**
   - Work with the DLP team to obtain full content inspection of what was uploaded.
   - Contact the AI provider's trust/security team to request data deletion (if the provider supports it).
   - Determine if the data was uploaded via web interface (may be used for training) or API (typically not used for training under business terms).

4. **Remediate:**
   - Conduct a user interview to understand intent and scope.
   - Enforce mandatory AI usage policy training for the user.
   - Implement DLP policies that block (not just monitor) uploads to AI services.
   - Review and strengthen proxy policies for AI domains.

5. **Document:**
   - Record the full incident timeline, data types involved, and response actions in the case management system.
   - If a regulatory notification is required, coordinate with Legal and Compliance.

### If Benign True Positive

1. Validate that the user is authorized and the data type does not violate policy.
2. Add the user to `ai_sanctioned_entities.csv` with appropriate scope and expiration.
3. Document the justification.
4. Close the notable as "Benign True Positive."

## Tuning Guidance

### Reducing false positives

- Coordinate with the DLP team to ensure destination fields are accurately populated in DLP events.
- If specific DLP policy types generate frequent noise (e.g., generic "data in motion" policies), consider filtering by `dlp_type` in the search.
- Ensure `ai_provider_domains.csv` does not contain overly broad domain matches.

### Analyzing DLP-AI overlap in your environment

```spl
| datamodel Data_Loss_Prevention search
| search DLP.dest="*"
| eval ai_domain=lower(coalesce(DLP.dest,""))
| rex field=ai_domain "(?i)^(?:[a-z][a-z0-9+.-]*://)?(?<clean_domain>[^/?#:]+)"
| eval clean_domain=coalesce(clean_domain,ai_domain)
| lookup ai_provider_domains domain as clean_domain OUTPUTNEW provider
| stats count by provider clean_domain
| sort -count
```

Use this query to understand which AI providers and domains appear in your DLP data. If certain providers generate excessive noise, consider adding them to the allowlist or adjusting DLP policies.

### Adding allowlist entries

To allowlist a specific user for DLP-AI correlation (use with extreme caution):
```
entity_type,entity,provider,scope,expires,owner,reason,enabled,allow_key
user,analyst@corp.com,OpenAI,provider,2026-06-30,security_lead,approved_ai_research_with_DLP_review,1,analyst@corp.com|openai
```
